Peter Diamandis, known for his futuristic vision and tech entrepreneurship, has released a new episode of his MOONSHOTS series that addresses one of the most thorny legal issues of our time: can an artificial intelligence be recognized as an inventor?

The video, titled 'Who Owns AI's Ideas?', explores the legal and ethical implications of intellectual property generated by AI systems. With the advancement of generative models, the question becomes increasingly practical: if an AI creates a new technology or an innovative process, who owns the patent?

Diamandis does not offer definitive answers but raises the central arguments of the debate. On one side, the traditional view of copyright and patent law requires 'human creativity' as a prerequisite. On the other, systems like DABUS (Device for the Autonomous Bootstrapping of Unified Sentience) have already had patent applications rejected in several countries precisely because they did not list a human as an inventor.

The most emblematic case is that of DABUS, created by Dr. Stephen Thaler. In 2021, Australia initially accepted that an AI could be an inventor, but the decision was later reversed. In the US and Europe, courts have maintained the requirement of a human inventor.

For AIatolah, this video is relevant because it touches on the core of the relationship between technological innovation and the legal framework. As models like GPT-4 and others generate code, molecules, and designs, the legal system needs to adapt. The discussion is not merely academic: companies using AI for R&D may face uncertainties about protecting their results.

Diamandis suggests that without a clear definition, innovation could be slowed down. If AI-generated patents are not protected, the incentive to invest in automated research diminishes.

The video is a good starting point for anyone wanting to understand the contours of this debate. The production is clear and straightforward, without apocalyptic exaggerations — aligned with AIatolah's editorial line.
