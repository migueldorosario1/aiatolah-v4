Peter Diamandis, known for his optimistic vision of exponential technology, has released a new video in his MOONSHOTS series. The theme: AI is beginning to design better AI hardware than humans.

The central argument is straightforward but profound in scope. If AI can optimize the design of chips and computer architectures, it accelerates its own physical substrate. This creates a positive feedback loop: faster hardware enables smarter models, which design even faster hardware.

Diamandis calls this one of the greatest accelerators of recursive improvement. The concept is not new in theory, but the practice is solidifying. Companies like Google and NVIDIA already use reinforcement learning to find more efficient transistor layouts. What was once a manual process taking years can now be compressed into weeks.

The video explores the implications for Moore's Law, which has already slowed in terms of transistor density. AI can extend or redefine this curve, not through finer lithography, but through radically new architectures. Diamandis suggests we are entering an era of 'design by discovery', where the machine finds solutions no human engineer would consider.

For the AIatolah editorial line, this video is relevant because it connects hardware, software, and geopolitics. Whoever masters AI-assisted chip design will have a decisive advantage in the race between the US and China. Moreover, it raises questions about control: if AI designs its own brain, who holds the roadmap?

Watch the video for a condensed but impactful view of how recursive self-improvement can move from paper into fab labs.
