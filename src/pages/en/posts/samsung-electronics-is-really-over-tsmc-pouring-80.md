Samsung Electronics is facing a moment of truth as TSMC pours 80 trillion won into AI semiconductor foundry dominance, a move that signals a brutal shakeup in the global chip industry. The Korean giant, once the undisputed king of memory chips, now finds itself scrambling to catch up in the high-stakes race for artificial intelligence hardware.

TSMC’s massive investment is not just about money; it is a declaration of war aimed at sweeping the entire AI foundry market, leaving Samsung with no room for error. Samsung’s foundry business has struggled with low yields and a lack of major AI clients, while TSMC has locked in orders from Nvidia, AMD, and Apple for years to come.

The 80 trillion won figure is staggering, representing TSMC’s bet that AI chips will be the most critical technology of the next decade, outpacing even smartphones and PCs. Samsung, meanwhile, is pouring its own billions into advanced 3nm and 2nm processes, but it has yet to land a single high-volume AI chip deal from a top-tier designer.

This gap is not just about technology; it is about trust and ecosystem, as TSMC has built a seamless partnership with chip designers that Samsung cannot easily replicate. Without a major AI client like Nvidia or Qualcomm, Samsung’s foundry risks becoming a second-tier player, even as its memory business booms from AI demand.

The Korean government is now stepping in with aggressive subsidies and tax breaks, hoping to revive Samsung’s competitive edge before it is too late. But the reality is that TSMC’s head start in advanced packaging and chiplet integration gives it an almost insurmountable lead in the AI foundry race.

For Samsung, the path forward is narrow: it must either win a flagship AI chip contract or pivot to specialize in niche markets like automotive and IoT. If it fails, the narrative of “Samsung Electronics Is Really Over” may not be hyperbole but a stark forecast of a once-dominant empire losing its crown.
