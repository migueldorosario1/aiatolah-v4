Samsung Electronics finds itself at a critical crossroads as TSMC tightens its grip on the global semiconductor foundry market. The Korean giant’s new strategy aims to exploit a rare opening in the “TSMC+α” era, where AI chip demand is shifting beyond standard manufacturing.

Samsung’s key counterattack plan revolves around its advanced Gate-All-Around (GAA) transistor technology, which promises better performance and energy efficiency than TSMC’s current FinFET designs. This technological leap could attract AI chip designers looking for an edge in the race to build faster, more powerful processors.

However, Samsung faces a steep uphill battle, as TSMC has already locked in major clients like NVIDIA and AMD for years to come. The Korean firm must also prove it can deliver consistent yields and reliable supply chains, areas where TSMC has long excelled.

The rise of Chinese AI chip startups, such as those using DeepSeek and Kimi models, adds another layer of complexity to the market. These new players are hungry for advanced manufacturing but may prefer TSMC’s proven stability over Samsung’s unproven GAA process.

Samsung’s best chance lies in targeting niche AI applications, like edge computing and specialized accelerators, where TSMC’s dominance is less absolute. By offering flexible pricing and faster turnaround times, Samsung could win over smaller, agile chip designers.

The outcome of this foundry war will shape not just Korea’s AI ambitions, but the entire global semiconductor landscape. For now, Samsung must execute its GAA strategy flawlessly to turn this counterattack into a lasting comeback.
