China’s push to localize 50% of its semiconductor supply chain is reshaping the global tech landscape, forcing Korean equipment makers to seek survival paths in Taiwan. This strategic move aims to reduce dependency on foreign chips and strengthen national tech sovereignty amid escalating US-China tensions.

Korean companies like Hanmi Semiconductor and Wonik IPS are now pivoting to Taiwan, where demand for advanced chipmaking tools remains high due to TSMC’s expansion. These firms see Taiwan as a stable alternative to China’s volatile market, leveraging their expertise in memory and logic chip production equipment.

The localization drive has already spurred Chinese firms like DeepSeek and Qwen to develop AI models optimized for domestic hardware, challenging Western giants like Nvidia. Kimi, another Chinese AI startup, is also integrating local chips to power its language models, reducing reliance on US-made processors.

However, China’s goal of 50% self-sufficiency by 2025 faces hurdles, as its domestic chipmakers still lag in cutting-edge nodes like 3nm and 5nm. Korean equipment makers, meanwhile, are betting on Taiwan’s mature processes and advanced packaging to sustain revenue streams.

This shift highlights a broader geopolitical realignment, where technology supply chains are fragmenting into competing blocs. For Korea, the move to Taiwan is a pragmatic hedge, allowing its companies to serve both Chinese and Western markets without full alignment with either side.

Ultimately, China’s localization push and Korea’s Taiwan pivot underscore the AI-driven race for chip independence. As DeepSeek and Kimi advance, the global semiconductor order will increasingly reflect a multipolar world, where innovation and geopolitics are deeply intertwined.
